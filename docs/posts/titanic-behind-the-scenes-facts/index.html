James Cameron Blew The Budget, Nearly Killed His Cast, And Then Spent 2023 Fighting About A Door

The making of Titanic was basically its own disaster movie — freezing water, budget meltdowns, PCP-laced chowder, and one very stubborn director defending a piece of wood paneling for 25 years.

1

Two Studios, One Panicking Budget, And A CEO Quietly Bracing For A $55 Million Bath

Titanic's budget ballooned so badly that Fox had to beg Paramount to co-finance it just to survive — and even then, Fox's own CEO was privately preparing for the worst. The film's price tag exploded from an original estimate of around $109 million to a jaw-dropping $200+ million, making it the most expensive movie ever made at the time.

2

Cameron Dove To The Actual Titanic Wreck 12 Times Before He Even Wrote The Script

This wasn't a metaphor — Cameron and his crew shot real footage of the RMS Titanic wreck on the ocean floor starting in September 1995, before a single page of the screenplay existed. He later admitted the real motive was less about the movie and more about the dive itself.

3

Kate Winslet Got Hypothermia (Not Pneumonia) — And Almost Drowned Twice

Despite decades of rumors that Winslet caught pneumonia filming the water scenes, she later clarified the truth was hypothermia — made worse by her refusal to wear a wetsuit. She also nearly drowned when her heavy coat snagged on a gate during a flooding-corridor scene, swallowing mouthfuls of water before crew could free her.

4

Cameron Spent 2023 Running A Literal Science Experiment To Win The Door Argument

Twenty-five years after fans started arguing that Jack could've survived by squeezing onto the door with Rose, Cameron got a hypothermia expert and stunt doubles to recreate the raft scene for a National Geographic special. The verdict: across four tests, he concluded the floating debris (technically wood paneling, not a door) couldn't have kept them both alive — though he admitted one scenario showed Jack 'might have lived.'

5

Someone Spiked The Crew's Clam Chowder With PCP And Sent 80 People To The ER

During filming in Nova Scotia, dozens of cast and crew members — including Bill Paxton and Cameron himself — ate lobster chowder that had secretly been laced with PCP, and were rushed to a small local hospital assuming it was food poisoning. Cameron has described the scene as people wailing and collapsed on gurneys while the cinematographer led a conga line down the hallway, and to this day nobody has ever been charged.
